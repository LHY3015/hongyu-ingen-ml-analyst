# Literature review summary   
## Abstract

This literature review surveys five research contributions directly relevant to the machine learning challenges encountered in deploying autonomous robotic systems at production scale. Drawing on InGen Dynamics' Origami AI platform and its Physical Intelligence Core (PIC 2.0) as a motivating context, I examine recent work on: (1) the broader landscape of embodied AI and robotic manipulation learning; (2)  sensor-based fault detection; (3) trajectory prediction via physics-informed graph neural ODE; (4) neural approaches to time-series anomaly detection; and (5) deployment constraints for models on embedded edge hardware. Taken together, these works delineate the core technical challenges — sensor reliability, motion forecasting, uncertainty estimation, and real-time inference — that any production physical AI system must resolve. 

---

## 1. Introduction

The emergence of physical AI — autonomous systems that perceive, reason, and act in the real world — represents one of the most demanding settings for applied machine learning [1]. Unlike software-only AI, physical systems operate under tight constraints on latency, sensor noise, environmental uncertainty, and safety, while being required to perform reliably across thousands of cumulative operating hours. 

InGen Dynamics' Origami AI platform illustrates both the ambition and the complexity of this challenge. PIC 2.0, the platform's cognitive pipeline, integrates six foundation models — GRPO for policy optimisation, STUM for spatiotemporal uncertainty estimation, SEOM for ethical safety oversight, AMDC for sensor calibration and drift compensation, HTD-IRL for hierarchical task decomposition, and CRL-MRS for multi-robot fleet coordination. This  structure has been deployed across many platforms (Fari for eldercare, Senpai for  education, Sentinel Prime AI for security, Aido Rover for outdoor patrol, and Aido Humanoid for dexterous manipulation).[1]

Achieving these results requires solutions to research problems that remain active: how to detect anomalies and failures; how to predict robot and human trajectory under physical constraints; how to identify distribution shifts and anomalous states in streaming sensor time series; and how to deploy large learned models within the latency and power budgets imposed by edge hardware. The following five sections review recent work addressing each of these problems, situating the contributions in the context of InGen's deployment constraints.

## 2. Embodied AI and Object-Centric Robotic Manipulation: A Survey

### 2.1 Overview

Zheng et al. [2] provide a comprehensive survey of embodied learning for object-centric robotic manipulation. The survey covers representation learning, skill acquisition, multi-task generalisation, and the role of simulation-to-real transfer, synthesising over several hundred prior works across the 2018–2024 period.

### 2.2 Key Contributions

The survey organises the field around four axes: (a) **perception**, which encompasses how robots build object-level representations from visual, tactile, and proprioceptive signals; (b) **skill learning**, covering imitation learning (IL), reinforcement learning (RL), and hybrid IL+RL approaches; (c) **generalisation**, including few-shot and zero-shot transfer to novel objects and environments; and (d) **evaluation**, where the authors identify a lack of standardised benchmarks as a significant obstacle to progress.

A central observation is the growing dominance of transformer-based architectures for policy learning. Vision-language-action (VLA) models, which condition low-level motor actions on natural language task descriptions and visual observations, are identified as the current frontier — capable of zero-shot generalisation across robot morphologies when trained on sufficiently large and diverse demonstration datasets. The survey also identifies constitutional safety constraints as an underexplored area: most academic manipulation systems assume a benign environment and impose no explicit constraints on the learned policy during training.

### 2.3 Relevance to PIC 2.0

The survey's taxonomy maps directly onto PIC 2.0's structure. GRPO instantiates the RL skill-learning paradigm with a group-relative advantage estimate, $A_i = r_{\text{rel}}(\tau_i) / \sigma_{\text{group}}$, which improves sample efficiency relative to standard policy gradient methods. HTD-IRL addresses the generalisation problem by learning hierarchical task decompositions from expert demonstrations via inverse RL, rather than relying on hand-coded action primitives. The gap Zheng et al. identify around safety constraints is directly filled by SEOM, which embeds domain-specific rules as differentiable penalty gradients during training, giving the policy a constitutional incapacity to execute unsafe actions. 

## 3. Multi-modal Sensor Fusion and Failure Detection in Robot Manipulation

### 3.1 Overview

Inceoglu, Aksoy, and Sariel [3] propose FINO-Net (*Failure Identification Network*), a deep multimodal sensor fusion architecture for detecting and classifying robot manipulation failures in real time. Published in *IEEE Robotics and Automation Letters* (Vol. 9, No. 2, 2024), the work addresses the problem of identifying failures — grasping misses, dropped objects, collisions — from raw, unannotated sensory streams during and after manipulation tasks.

### 3.2 Key Contributions

FINO-Net accepts heterogeneous sensor inputs (RGB images, force/torque readings, joint encoders, and proprioceptive state) and processes them through modality-specific encoders, followed by cross-modal attention fusion. A key design decision is operating on raw sensory data without requiring any explicit task description, scene state, or object model. This is motivated by the practical reality that real manipulators operate in partially observable environments where ground-truth state is unavailable.

The model achieves an F1 score of 0.87 for failure detection and 0.80 for failure classification across a diverse set of manipulation failure types, and the authors demonstrate that FINO-Net meets the latency requirements for real-time deployment (inference runs in under 12 ms on an NVIDIA Jetson Xavier). The cross-modal attention mechanism proves crucial: fusion at the feature level outperforms late fusion (combining per-modality predictions) by a statistically significant margin, suggesting that failure signatures are distributed across sensory modalities and not recoverable from any single stream.

### 3.3 Relevance to PIC 2.0

FINO-Net's architecture is directly analogous to AMDC (Adaptive Multi-Domain Calibration) module. In AMDC, cross-sensor consistency is monitored continuously; deviation of one sensor from a calibrated ensemble model triggers a re-calibration cycle rather than propagating an erroneous reading downstream. In Fari, for example, AMDC corrects rPPG heart rate readings for ambient light variation — an analogous problem to FINO-Net's requirement to distinguish genuine manipulation failures from sensor-level artifacts. The FINO-Net result that failure classification requires multi-modal fusion corroborates InGen's architectural choice to run all sensor streams through a unified calibration pipeline before any downstream reasoning. It also identifies a productive direction: AMDC currently operates per-platform; a cross-platform version with shared failure taxonomy could leverage FINO-Net's insight that failure modes generalize across task types.

## 4. Trajectory Prediction with Physics-Informed Graph Neural Ordinary Differential Equations(ODE)

### 4.1 Overview

Mo, Fu, and Di [4] present PI-NeuGODE (*Physics-Informed Graph Neural Ordinary Differential Equations*), a spatiotemporal trajectory prediction framework published at the 23rd International Conference on Autonomous Agents and Multiagent Systems (AAMAS 2024). The work addresses multi-agent trajectory forecasting — predicting the future positions of multiple interacting agents (robots, people, vehicles) simultaneously — under physics constraints.

### 4.2 Key Contributions

PI-NeuGODE combines two complementary components: a graph neural network (GNN) for modelling spatial interactions among agents, and a neural ordinary differential equation (Neural ODE) for modelling the continuous-time temporal dynamics of each agent's trajectory. Critically, the authors incorporate physics-informed constraints — conservation of momentum, smoothness priors, and collision-avoidance penalties — directly into the ODE right-hand side. This produces trajectories that are simultaneously data-driven (learned from observed motion histories) and physically plausible (free of dynamically infeasible predictions such as instant velocity reversals).

The framework outperforms LSTM-based and standard GNN baselines on standard benchmarks in both minimum final displacement error (minFDE) and collision rate. The Neural ODE formulation is especially important for irregularly sampled trajectories — a common occurrence in multi-robot systems where communication dropouts produce missing observations — because it can integrate over arbitrary time intervals without retraining.

### 4.3 Relevance to PIC 2.0

Path planning in InGen's HTD-IRL and inter-robot conflict avoidance in CRL-MRS both require predictions of where other agents will be over a short planning horizon. PI-NeuGODE's physics-informed formulation is directly applicable here: the physical constraint that two robots cannot occupy the same space is a natural hard constraint in the ODE, and the irregularly sampled setting applies whenever a robot's state is temporarily unobservable due to occlusion or radio dropout. Integrating a PI-NeuGODE-style prediction component into CRL-MRS's planning module could replace the current lookup-table-based deadlock heuristics with principled probabilistic predictions, potentially further reducing the conflict penalty term $-\beta \cdot R_{\text{conflict}}$ in the cooperative reward $R_{\text{meta}} = R_{\text{task}} + \alpha R_{\text{cooperation}} - \beta R_{\text{conflict}}$.

## 5. Diffusion-Based Decomposition for Multivariate Time-Series Anomaly Detection

### 5.1 Overview

Wang et al. [5] present D³R (*Dynamic Decomposition with Diffusion Reconstruction*), a neural framework for unsupervised anomaly detection in multivariate time series. The work targets real-world industrial sensor streams, which are characterised by non-stationarity — gradual drift in the normal signal distribution — rather than the stable baselines assumed by most prior anomaly detection methods.

### 5.2 Key Contributions

D³R makes two principal contributions. First, a *data-time mix-attention* mechanism dynamically decomposes a multivariate time series into trend and residual components, adapting the decomposition basis as the distribution shifts over time. This addresses the core limitation of static decomposition methods (such as classical STL decomposition), which misidentify trend changes as anomalies when the background distribution drifts. Second, a *noise diffusion reconstruction* module uses a diffusion model to reconstruct the residual component from a noise-corrupted version, controlling the information bottleneck externally via the noise schedule. Anomalies are identified as regions where the reconstruction error exceeds a conformal prediction-calibrated threshold.

D³R achieves state-of-the-art results on five standard multivariate anomaly detection benchmarks (MSL, SMAP, SMD, SWaT, PSM), reducing false positive rates by a statistically significant margin relative to prior transformer-based methods, while remaining robust to the kind of slow distributional drift that causes competitor methods to degrade over multi-week deployments.

### 5.3 Relevance to PIC 2.0

STUM is InGen's primary mechanism for detecting anomalous sensor states and gating downstream decisions based on uncertainty. Its uncertainty estimate $\sigma_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{\sigma_{\text{spatial}}^2 + \sigma_{\text{temporal}}^2}$ captures both ensemble disagreement and temporal staleness, but it does not explicitly model non-stationarity in the sensor signal distribution. Over multi-week deployments — for example, Sentinel units operating across day/night cycles, seasonal temperature variations, and gradual lens fouling — the background distribution of normal observations drifts, which can inflate $\sigma_{\text{spatial}}$ and increase the frequency of operator review escalations unrelated to genuine security events. D³R's dynamic decomposition approach offers a solution: separating long-term trend from residual variation before uncertainty scoring would allow STUM to maintain a stable false-alert rate even as sensor baselines drift. Additionally, D³R's conformal prediction-based threshold calibration is methodologically consistent with STUM's existing use of conformal prediction for coverage bounds, suggesting possible architectural integration.

## 6. Deployment Constraints for Embodied Foundation Models at the Edge

### 6.1 Overview

[6] surveys the deployment of foundation models on embedded edge hardware for robotics applications. The work provides a systematic taxonomy of deployment constraints, organizing them into eight coupled barriers — *memory capacity*, *memory bandwidth*, *compute throughput*, *compute latency*, *power envelope*, *timing determinism*, *communication reliability*, and *safety margin* — and characterises the mitigation landscape across compression, hardware co-design, and architectural decomposition strategies.

### 6.2 Key Contributions

The survey's most operationally significant finding is a model-class asymmetry in dominant constraints: autoregressive VLA policies are primarily bottlenecked by *memory bandwidth*, because the sequential token generation process requires continuous weight streaming that saturates the memory bus long before compute units are fully utilised. Diffusion-based control policies (e.g., diffusion policy variants), by contrast, are primarily bottlenecked by *compute latency*, because the iterative denoising process requires many sequential forward passes that cannot be trivially parallelised on fixed-function edge accelerators.

The survey also introduces a *Deployment Gauntlet* framework, which models the eight barriers as a cascade: a system must clear all eight to achieve reliable deployment. Partial compliance causes intermittent failures that are difficult to diagnose because they are non-deterministic. The mitigation analysis concludes that reliable edge deployment of foundation-model-scale policies requires system-level co-design — architecture decisions must account for hardware topology from the outset, rather than retrofitting a trained model into an existing deployment stack.

### 6.3 Relevance to PIC 2.0

InGen's deployment target — full PIC 2.0 cycle on edge, makes the Deployment Gauntlet framework directly applicable. PIC 2.0's modular architecture, where GRPO handles policy generation while SEOM provides the safety gate, corresponds to the survey's recommended decomposition strategy: separating fast safety-critical control from slower semantic reasoning. The distillation pipeline addresses the compute throughput barrier, while the on-device-only safety chain addresses the timing determinism barrier . The survey's finding that auto regressive policies are memory-bandwidth-limited is particularly relevant to PIC 2.0's GRPO module, which uses group-relative estimation over N=16 to 24 sampled trajectories; batched parallel sampling during inference could alleviate the bandwidth bottleneck relative to sequential autoregressive decoding. 

## 7. Synthesis and Discussion

Considered together, these five works trace a coherent technical arc from *perception* to *decision* to *deployment*, precisely corresponding to the challenges that a system like PIC 2.0 must solve at each layer.

At the **sensor layer**, FINO-Net [3] establishes that multimodal fusion with cross-attention is necessary for reliable failure detection — a finding consistent with AMDC's design, which fuses up to 14 heterogeneous sensor streams before any downstream reasoning. D³R [5] adds the temporal dimension: anomaly detection in sensor streams must account for distributional drift, or false-alarm rates will degrade over long deployments, threatening the 2.8% false-alert target InGen sustains in Sentinel.

At the **prediction layer**, PI-NeuGODE [4] demonstrates that physics-informed continuous-time trajectory models outperform discrete-step recurrent approaches for multi-agent motion forecasting. This has direct implications for CRL-MRS: replacing heuristic collision avoidance with learned, physics-constrained trajectory prediction could make fleet coordination more robust to novel spatial configurations not seen during training.

At the **learning and architecture layer**, Zheng et al. [2] provide the survey context establishing that constitutional safety constraints remain an open research problem in the embodied AI community — a problem that SEOM component directly addresses through differentiable safety gradients. The field broadly lacks SEOM-equivalent mechanisms, meaning InGen's approach represents a research contribution relative to the academic state of the art, not merely an engineering choice.

At the **deployment layer**, [6] supplies the systems framing: architectural modularity, distillation, and hardware-aware co-design are not optional optimisations but necessary conditions for reliable edge deployment. The Deployment Gauntlet maps onto the PIC 2.0 pipeline constraint-by-constraint, confirming that InGen's design choices are aligned with the same constraints the survey identifies as fundamental.

## 8. Conclusion

This review has examined five recent high-impact works in the areas of physical AI and robotics ML, sensor-based fault detection, trajectory prediction, time-series anomaly detection, and edge deployment constraints. Each work illuminates a specific technical challenge that manifests concretely in PIC 2.0 architecture. Taken together, they suggest some possible near-term directions for production physical AI systems: (a) integrating dynamic anomaly detection (D³R-style) with calibrated uncertainty estimation (STUM) for long-horizon deployment stability; (b) replacing heuristic multi-agent planning with learned physics-informed forecasting (PI-NeuGODE) in fleet coordination modules; and (c) systematically applying hardware-aware co-design from the start of model architecture development, rather than as a post-training optimisation pass.
